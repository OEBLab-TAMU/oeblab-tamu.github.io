Research

Our Research Areas

Lab and field-based studies investigating musculoskeletal injury mechanisms and developing evidence-based interventions across diverse occupational settings.

Research Area 1

⚓ Commerical Fishing Health and Safety

Commercial fishing is the most hazardous occupation in the United States. While prior studies focus on fatal injuries, our research targets nonfatal musculoskeletal disorders in the upper extremities and low back during crab pot handling. We quantify biomechanical risk and evaluate various innovative interventions including exoskeletons and fishermen-led engineering controls.

Research Objectives

  • →Characterize musculoskeletal injury risk associated with commercial fishing operations
  • →Quantify impact of commercial fishing activities on biomechanical risk and postural balance (fall risk)
  • →Evaluate efficacy of innovative interventions such exoskeletons in reducing physical risk factors
  • →Deploy wearable sensors for feasibility studies in authentic commercial fishing environments
Sponsors: National Institute of Occuaptional Safety and Health/U.S. Coastal Guard (NIOSH/USCG)
Research Area 2

🦾 Exoskeletons Research

We evaluate various passive and active exoskeletons to reduce biomechanical loading and improve occupational health across multiple industries including commercial fishing, foresry, construction and healthcare. The evaluations are conducted in both controlled laboratory settings and real field settings.

Research Objectives

  • →Evaluate passive upper-limb exoskeletons for reducing shoulder/neck strain in VR/AR
  • →Assess back-support exoskeletons for forestry, commerical fishing, agriculture, and construction workers in field conditions
  • →Evaluate exoskeletons for electrical workers operating scissor lifts in dynamic conditions
  • →Develop criteria for selecting and implementing exoskeletons in hazardous occupations
Sponsors: National Institute of Occuaptional Safety and Health/U.S. Coastal Guard (NIOSH/USCG), National Science Foundation (NSF)

Research Area 1

🚛 Whole Body Vibration in Professional Drivers

Exposure to whole body vibration (WBV) has a strong association with musculoskeletal disorders, especially low back pain. Through lab- and field-based research, we investigate the effect of multi-axial vibration on vehicle operators using a 6-DOF motion platform that replicates real field-measured vibration.

Research Objectives

  • →Characterize multi-axial WBV exposures among professional drivers in real field conditions
  • →Evaluate passive and active seat suspension systems for reducing vibration transmission
  • →Assess biomechanical and physiological effects of vibration on spine loading and muscle fatigue
  • →Conduct RCTs evaluating engineering interventions on driver low back pain

    Research Area 2

    💻 Computer-Human Interaction & VR/AR Ergonomics

    By evaluating biomechanical exposures and usability in computer and VR/AR work, we explore opportunities to reduce work-related musculoskeletal disorders. VR/AR interactions often require large upper extremity movements without adequate postural support, posing significant shoulder and neck strain risks.

    Research Objectives

    • →Quantify neck and shoulder biomechanical loads during VR/AR and computer interactions
    • →Investigate effects of target size and error rate on cognitive demand and musculoskeletal strain
    • →Evaluate passive exoskeletons for reducing shoulder/neck strain during prolonged VR/AR use
    • →Develop evidence-based design guidelines for safer VR/AR and computer workstations

    Research Area 4

    🏥 Healthcare Ergonomics

    Caregivers face a high prevalence of musculoskeletal injuries, particularly in the low back and shoulders, creating an estimated $1.6 billion annual economic burden in the US. Our research evaluates assistive devices and workflow modifications to reduce injury risk during patient handling tasks.

    Research Objectives

    • →Quantify biomechanical loading in the low back and upper extremities during patient handling
    • →Evaluate air-assisted and mechanical devices for reducing caregiver musculoskeletal exposure
    • →Assess patient repositioning and transfer tasks for biomechanical risk factors
    • →Develop evidence-based safe patient handling guidelines for healthcare institutions

    Research Area 5

    ⚓ Commercial Fishing Ergonomics & Safety

    Commercial fishing is the most hazardous occupation in the United States. While prior studies focus on fatal injuries, our research targets nonfatal musculoskeletal disorders in the upper extremities and low back during crab pot handling. We quantify biomechanical risk and evaluate the fishermen-led "banger bar" engineering control.

    Research Objectives

    • →Characterize musculoskeletal injury risk during Dungeness crab pot handling operations
    • →Quantify impact of crab pot handling on biomechanical risk and postural balance (fall risk)
    • →Evaluate efficacy of the banger bar engineering control in reducing physical risk factors
    • →Deploy wearable sensors for feasibility studies in authentic commercial fishing environments
